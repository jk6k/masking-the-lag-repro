\begin{abstract}
Dynamic photonic tensor cores can accelerate Transformer attention and
feed-forward kernels, but their system-level benefit is often constrained by
data conversion, memory movement, operand broadcast, and the absence of a clean
path from the software scheduler to accelerator quality controls. This paper
presents SUDS, a scheduler-derived budget interface for dynamic photonic
Transformer accelerators. SUDS exposes timing slack as KEEP, DEGRADE, and PRUNE
budgets for Lightening-style photonic tensor-core tiles, then lets local L1 or
signal-amplitude selectors spend those budgets on converter precision,
degradation, and pruning decisions. The architecture model maps BERT-base GLUE
encoder kernels and MobileViT-S Transformer blocks to four-tile, two-core
optical arrays while accounting for optical compute, electro-optic conversion,
memory traffic, optical-link cost, sideband control, and calibrated sensitivity
settings. Across two Transformer workloads, the selected operating point links
local MPS accuracy runs to modeled system metrics and preserves at least
14.60\% pessimistic EDP improvement over the same-scope Lightening-style
reference. The claim is therefore architecture-simulation evidence, linked to
measured local accuracy and calibration data; it is not a fabrication, layout,
foundry, circuit-signoff, optical-device-signoff, or bench-energy claim.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Transformer accelerators based on dynamic photonic tensor cores promise high
throughput because attention and feed-forward layers are dominated by matrix
multiplication in modern Transformer workloads and benchmarks
\cite{vaswani2017attention,devlin2019bert,wang2019glue,mehta2022mobilevit,dosovitskiy2021vit}.
A Lightening-style fabric is a useful reference because it
supports dynamic full-range matrix products, not only weight-static optical
tensors \cite{lightening2024}. HyAtten-style work highlights a complementary
bottleneck: even when
the optical core is fast, conversion can dominate the end-to-end cost, so
low-resolution readout and local signal selection matter \cite{hyatten2025}.
What is still missing is a control interface between these hardware knobs and
the schedule, which already knows when a kernel has timing slack.

SUDS turns that timing information into an explicit resource budget. A
scheduler exports slack to the accelerator control plane; the photonic tile
controller converts it into KEEP, DEGRADE, and PRUNE counts; and local selectors
choose the exact columns inside those counts. This distinction is central to
the paper. SUDS is not a claim that slack alone is a semantic importance
oracle. Instead, it combines scheduler pressure with local L1,
signal-amplitude, or overflow-style selectors.
Fig.~\ref{fig:timing-slack-bottleneck} gives the motivation for converting
otherwise idle slack into an explicit hardware budget. The architecture and
budgeting mechanism are detailed in Fig.~\ref{fig:suds-microarchitecture} and
Fig.~\ref{fig:suds-budget-interface}.

The scope is deliberately architecture-first. The paper models the accelerator
system, links selected rows to measured local accuracy runs, and uses
calibration evidence to parameterize and stress the model without turning it
into a hardware signoff claim.

\subsection{Transformer Kernel Mapping}

The simulator represents each Transformer operation as a tiled matrix
multiplication on the selected photonic array. BERT-base uses the 12-layer
encoder at sequence length 128 and includes QKV projection, QK score,
attention-value, output projection, and two feed-forward kernels per layer.
MobileViT-S uses the repository's Transformer-block operation manifest and
includes the linear attention and feed-forward kernels under
\texttt{global\_rep}. Each matrix multiplication is lowered into tile work, and
the model accumulates latency and energy from optical compute, DAC/MZM input
conversion, detector/TIA readout, ADC conversion, laser power, SRAM movement,
optical-link movement, and sideband-control groups.

\subsection{Selected Operating Point}

The selected operating point intentionally stays close to the Lightening and
HyAtten comparison surface. It uses 32 by 32 photonic tensor-core tiles, four
tiles, two cores per tile, 32-column sideband groups, and temporal accumulation
before conversion. The design-space sweep covers 4536 configurations and
identifies 594 Pareto rows over raw energy, latency, and area. The chosen point
is not an unconstrained optimum over those raw metrics. It is retained after
the one-percentage-point accuracy budget, same-simulator baseline comparison,
memory-pressure objective, control-overhead objective, and
calibration-sensitivity objective are applied. Table~\ref{tab:operating-point}
records this setting. Neighboring designs that improve a raw system metric
remain visible in the design-space data, but they do not replace the selected
point because the paper evaluates a constrained operating point rather than

\subsection{SUDS Control Layer}

For each scheduled kernel, the simulator records issue time, tile latency,
downstream deadline, and normalized slack. Low slack keeps high-resolution
conversion, medium slack permits lower-resolution readout, and high slack can
permit pruning or stronger degradation when the measured workload surface
allows it. The L1 and signal-composed variants keep the scheduler-derived tier
counts but use local selectors to choose exact columns. The SUDS-only ablation
isolates the budget interface from the local selector. Only the
schedule-guarded \texttt{suds\_pareto} row is used as the main result. More
aggressive composed rows remain visible as ablations and boundary context.

\subsection{Pessimistic Stress Test}

The fixed architecture stress test varies converter scaling, memory movement,
optical-link penalties, control overhead, and reduced compute relief from
pruning. Under that setting, the selected point preserves 22.36\% energy and
25.81\% EDP improvement for BERT-base GLUE, and 14.75\% energy and 14.60\%
EDP improvement for MobileViT-S Transformer blocks, relative to the same-scope
Lightening-style reference. A broader sensitivity sweep then varies memory
bandwidth, activation reuse, batch size, sequence length, converter cost, laser
multiplier, optical-link loss, and sideband overhead. The named pessimistic
regime preserves at least 12.10\% EDP improvement, while extreme one-at-a-time
DAC/MZM scaling exposes the MobileViT-S boundary where the selected point is no
longer beneficial. Combined stress grids over conversion, link, memory,
sequence length, converter energy, and sideband overhead are used to mark valid
regions and failure boundaries rather than to define realistic operating
regimes. Monte Carlo analysis over target-regime system parameters and
threshold jitter keeps the 95\% EDP improvement interval positive for both
selected workloads. Boundary draws and counterexample rows identify when the
interface should be disabled, treated only as simulator evidence, or paired with
stronger local-selector context.

\section{Related Work and Boundary Comparison}

SUDS is closest to dynamic photonic Transformer acceleration, but it targets a
different control layer. Lightening-Transformer supplies the same-scope dynamic
full-range DPTC reference, while HyAtten exposes how conversion and
low-resolution readout can dominate end-to-end attention acceleration
\cite{lightening2024,hyatten2025}. TeMPO and ASTRA are retained as
alternate-fabric boundaries because they change the readout or stochastic
compute assumptions rather than only adding a scheduler-derived budget
\cite{tempo2024,astra2025}. ENLighten changes model structure through
photonic-aware compression, Optical Transformers study optical scaling and
noise tolerance, and TRON represents non-coherent silicon-photonic Transformer
acceleration \cite{enlighten2025,opticaltransformers2023,tron2023}. Earlier
photonic neural accelerators, photonic-AI surveys, and linear-optical mesh
foundations provide context for the converter, MAC, and mesh assumptions but are
not same-scope scheduler interfaces
\cite{heana2024,crosslight2021,sonic2021,holylight2019,shastri2021photonics,nahmias2020photonics,clements2016mesh,reck1994unitary}.

\section{Problem Statement and Evidence Boundary}

\subsection{Core Research Question}

The core research question is: given scheduler slack, a Transformer kernel
DAG, and the current state of a DPTC-style photonic tile array, can the control
plane choose KEEP, DEGRADE, and PRUNE budgets that minimize modeled system
energy while satisfying kernel deadlines, resource constraints, calibration
boundaries, and measured accuracy guards?

This question intentionally defines SUDS as an architecture interface rather
than as an empirical selector rule. The scheduler supplies deadline pressure,
SUDS emits a budget, and a local selector spends that budget on exact columns.
The output of SUDS is therefore a budget vector, not a final mask.

\subsection{Deadline-Constrained Energy Minimization}

Let \(G=(V,E)\) be the scheduled Transformer kernel DAG. Each node
\(i \in V\) is a GEMM-like kernel with dimensions \(m_i,d_i,n_i\), release
time \(r_i\), deadline \(D_i\), normalized scheduler slack \(\sigma_i\), and
a controllable set of \(C_i\) output columns or column groups. Let \(X_t\)
denote the state of tile resource \(t\): tile dimension, tile availability,
core count, sideband grouping, ADC-sharing mode, memory/link state, and
calibrated cost parameters. SUDS chooses
\[
B_i = (N_i^{\mathrm{K}},N_i^{\mathrm{D}},N_i^{\mathrm{P}}),
\]
the KEEP, DEGRADE, and PRUNE budget for kernel \(i\). The local selector
\(\psi\) chooses the exact columns inside that budget.

The architecture problem is:
\begin{equation}
\min_{\{B_i,s_i,Y_i\}}
\sum_{i \in V} E_i(B_i,X_{Y_i},\mathcal{P})
\label{eq:suds-energy-objective}
\end{equation}
subject to the schedule, budget, and evidence constraints below. Here
\(s_i\) is the start time, \(Y_i\) is the tile-resource assignment, and
\(\mathcal{P}\) is the calibrated parameter set. The energy term includes ADC,
DAC/MZM, detector/TIA, laser, SRAM movement, optical-link movement,
sideband-control, and digital-fallback terms. The paper reports EDP as a
secondary metric, but the problem being solved is deadline-constrained energy
minimization.

\begin{align}
s_j &\ge s_i + L_i(B_i,X_{Y_i}), && \forall (i,j)\in E \label{eq:precedence}\\
s_i + L_i(B_i,X_{Y_i}) &\le D_i, && \forall i \in V \label{eq:deadline}\\
N_i^{\mathrm{K}}+N_i^{\mathrm{D}}+N_i^{\mathrm{P}} &= C_i, && \forall i \in V \label{eq:budget}\\
B_i &\in \mathcal{B}(\sigma_i,X_{Y_i},\mathcal{P}), && \forall i \in V \label{eq:slack-budget}\\
R_w(\{B_i\},\psi) &\le \epsilon_w, && \forall w \in \mathcal{W}_{\mathrm{prom}} .
\label{eq:accuracy-risk}
\end{align}

Equation~\ref{eq:precedence} preserves DAG order.
Equation~\ref{eq:deadline} is the deadline constraint.
Equation~\ref{eq:budget} makes the SUDS output a conserved budget over
columns or column groups.
Equation~\ref{eq:slack-budget} encodes the slack-to-budget policy: low slack
raises the required KEEP budget, while higher slack permits more DEGRADE or
PRUNE budget. If a workload evidence surface does not permit pruning, the
feasible set forces \(N_i^{\mathrm{P}}=0\). The selected MobileViT-S Pareto
point uses that zero-prune guard.
Equation~\ref{eq:accuracy-risk} prevents a workload from becoming a headline
result when its measured accuracy risk exceeds the workload guard. The full
variable-to-evidence table is in the supplement so the main paper can keep the
formal objective concise.

\subsection{Formal Input and Output}

The formal inputs are scheduler slack, kernel DAG, and tile state. Scheduler
slack carries deadline pressure after DPTC mapping; the kernel DAG carries
operation order, dimensions, release times, and deadlines; and tile state
captures available photonic resources, conversion sharing, sideband grouping,
memory/link state, and calibration parameters.

The formal output is the budget \(B_i\). KEEP preserves high-resolution
conversion. DEGRADE allows lower-resolution or degraded readout. PRUNE permits
removal or condensation only when the evidence surface supports it. The exact
column mask is deliberately outside the SUDS output contract and is produced by
the local selector inside the budget.

\subsection{Claim-to-Evidence Boundary}

The claim-to-evidence boundary is straightforward. SUDS is a
scheduler-derived budget interface; its input is slack plus DAG and tile state;
its output is a KEEP/DEGRADE/PRUNE budget rather than an exact mask; and its
selected rows combine modeled system PPA with measured local accuracy evidence.
Workload generality, ADC/RTL/PHY calibration, and alternate-fabric comparisons
remain boundary support, not stronger hardware or universal-transfer claims.

\subsection{Why a Scheduler Interface is Needed}

The motivating problem is not simply that optical multiplication can be fast.
A Transformer accelerator must also connect digital scheduling decisions to
photonic conversion resources. In a DPTC-style fabric, the optical datapath can
provide high arithmetic parallelism, yet conversion and memory terms can still
dominate the system-level benefit. The scheduler sees queueing pressure, layer
order, tile occupancy, and deadline slack; the converter-control plane sees
local column magnitudes and readout limits. Without an interface, these two
views remain separated. SUDS sits between them: it does not replace local
importance metrics, but restricts the budget that those metrics are allowed to
spend.

This distinction matters for the architecture claim.
If the method were presented as a pure slack selector, then a reviewer could
rightly ask why L1 or signal-only rows are not the main method.
If the method were presented as a pure signal selector, then the scheduler
would disappear and the architecture contribution would collapse into an
existing local heuristic.
The evidence supports a third position: the scheduler supplies a
hardware-derived budget, the local selector chooses columns inside that budget,
and the paper claims the interface and the measured operating point rather than
universal dominance over every local selector.

\subsection{Claim Units}

The paper reports claims at the level of an architecture row: a workload, a
photonic tensor-core operating point, a control policy, an accuracy source, and
a modeled system-accounting surface. These rows are not interchangeable. A
measured local accuracy row supports accuracy retention, while the architecture
model supports energy, latency, area, and EDP reasoning. Converter macro data,
control-plane synthesis proxies, and physical-link sweeps support parameter
choices and stress settings. None of these evidence sources, alone or together,
supports a fabricated-chip conclusion.

The main selected condition is \texttt{suds\_pareto}. For BERT-base GLUE, it
uses the measured zero-loss L1 condition under the DPTC schedule guard. For
MobileViT-S, it uses the measured no-prune signal-overflow condition with
\(\tau_{\mathrm{low}}=0.30\) and \(\tau_{\mathrm{high}}=0.95\). The older
\texttt{suds\_l1} and \texttt{suds\_signal} rows are retained as ablations
because they show what happens when the budget is more aggressive. They remain
ablations rather than main paper claims.

\subsection{Evidence Labels}

The manuscript uses explicit evidence labels so that different kinds of support
are not merged into a stronger conclusion than they can justify.
\texttt{measured\_mps\_glue} and \texttt{measured\_mps\_imagenet} mark GLUE
and MobileViT-S validation through the local MPS backend.
\texttt{modeled\_system\_ppa} marks energy, latency, area, and EDP values from
the architecture simulator.
\texttt{ngspice\_or\_fallback\_macro\_calibration} marks converter-tier values
used to calibrate the simulator.
\texttt{rtl\_synthesis\_proxy} ties sideband-control energy to a synthesis
proxy rather than to placed-and-routed timing closure.
\texttt{parametric\_boundary} marks stress settings bounded by sweeps rather
than by a physical implementation.

Together, these labels keep non-accuracy evidence in the calibration and
boundary category rather than recasting it as circuit closure or silicon
measurement.

\subsection{Non-Claims}

This work is not a fabricated-accelerator report: it does not include layout
timing, a foundry-backed tapeout, measured chip energy, a physical photonic
device experiment for SUDS, or circuit signoff. It also does not claim that
SUDS dominates every selector at every accuracy point, or that time-multiplexed
and stochastic photonic fabrics are same-scope with the selected DPTC point.
Any local internal review material is transparency support, not a substitute
for external peer review.

The contribution is narrower: a schedule-derived architecture interface with
measured accuracy linkage, matched same-fabric baselines, and a reproducible
evidence trail.

\section{Scheduler-Derived Metadata}

\subsection{Kernel Schedule Construction}

The BERT-base schedule is generated from the canonical encoder structure.
Each of the 12 layers contributes QKV projection, QK score, attention-value
context, attention output projection, FFN expansion, and FFN projection
kernels.
At sequence length 128 this creates a deterministic GEMM schedule.
The MobileViT-S schedule is extracted from the local operation manifest.
Only Transformer-block linear operations under the \texttt{global\_rep}
surface are included in the Transformer workload.
Convolutional support layers remain outside the selected Transformer-kernel
accounting.

For each matrix multiplication, the simulator records matrix dimensions
\(m\), \(d\), and \(n\). The mapping computes tile counts along the three
dimensions and derives output groups, output values, operand values, and base
latency. It also assigns a schedule start time and a downstream deadline. The
normalized slack value is then derived from the schedule window rather than
from an analytical layer index alone. The architecture JSON records the field
\texttt{slack\_source=dptc\_photonic\_tile\_schedule}.

\subsection{From Analytical Slack Records to Schedule Metadata}

The original GLUE measured record retains its analytical slack field. That
field is useful because it shows how the earlier GLUE rows were generated, but
it is not sufficient for the final architecture claim. The evidence chain
therefore adds a hardware-derived schedule link. The current architecture files
supply 1008 BERT schedule-kernel rows and 126 GLUE linkage rows, connecting the
measured GLUE rows to architecture conditions including
\texttt{suds\_pareto}. This keeps the data-generation history visible while
grounding the final claim in schedule metadata.

The scheduler-trace record strengthens that link by replaying the architecture
rows through five scheduler variants: FIFO, ASAP,
EDF/deadline-aware, utilization-aware, and SUDS-aware. The selected
\texttt{suds\_pareto} rows use SUDS-aware hardware-derived schedule trace
IDs \texttt{r2\_7b7427345a30be24} for BERT-base GLUE and
\texttt{r2\_e1d13a0a1855de8b} for MobileViT-S Transformer blocks. The same
record shows that scheduler distributions are distinguishable, with a
maximum signature distance of 0.566732 against a 0.010000 stop threshold. Thus
the selected slack source is an emitted scheduler trace, not only a column in
the architecture summary table.

A perturbation check then tests the accuracy side of that same link. It
connects the selected BERT-base GLUE and MobileViT-S
\texttt{suds\_pareto} rows to measured MPS forward passes, verifies that their
accuracy-source tier ratios match the architecture PPA tier ratios with maximum
policy-ratio delta 0.000000, and records the selected trace IDs
\texttt{r2\_7b7427345a30be24} and \texttt{r2\_e1d13a0a1855de8b}. The worst
selected accuracy delta is -0.014500 percentage points, inside the one
percentage-point target. The check remains deliberately bounded: it is a
model-forward tiered perturbation test, not a bit-exact ADC or optical-device
simulation.

\subsection{Schedule Metadata Fields}

The schedule metadata records the workload, kernel identifier and class, layer
index, matrix dimensions, tile counts, output groups, output values, base
latency, scheduled start, deadline, normalized slack, and mapping-evidence
field. The scheduler trace adds the scheduler variant, trace ID, dependency
kernels, release and data-ready times, completion time, deadline slack, queue
wait, tile assignment, resource-queue state, and KEEP/DEGRADE/PRUNE budget
class. These fields are sufficient to tie the emitted budget to the
selected photonic tensor-core schedule.

\subsection{Why the Schedule is Hardware-Derived}

The schedule is hardware-derived because slack is computed after mapping each
kernel to the selected tile shape, tile count, parallel-core count, sideband
grouping, and converter-sharing mode. Changing any of these choices changes
tile counts, parallelism, cycles, control accounting, or conversion timing and
energy. The resulting slack is therefore tied to the modeled architecture point
rather than to a static layer-order heuristic. The scheduler-trace study also
shows that scheduler choice changes the slack distribution: SUDS-aware traces
improve low-tail slack over FIFO for the selected rows, while a
utilization-aware scheduler has the highest p10 slack in the scheduler-only
ablation shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:scheduler-slack-budget}. The paper therefore
claims the scheduler-budget interface and the selected operating point, not
universal dominance by a single scheduling objective.

\section{SUDS Policy Definition}

\subsection{Budget Semantics}

SUDS maps normalized slack into three budget classes. KEEP preserves
high-resolution conversion, DEGRADE assigns lower-resolution conversion, and
PRUNE permits removal or condensation only when the measured workload surface
allows it. The selected MobileViT-S policy uses zero pruning: the earlier
aggressive policy reduced converter energy more strongly, but it lost too much
accuracy and was dominated by stronger local baselines. The conservative policy
therefore sacrifices some EDP improvement in exchange for a measured accuracy
delta near zero.

\subsection{Local Selector Composition}

The budget interface determines how many columns may be assigned to each tier;
the local selector determines which columns are assigned. L1 composition ranks
columns by weight magnitude, signal composition uses signal-amplitude or
overflow-style evidence, slack-only composition follows the schedule budget
directly, and random composition provides a same-sparsity boundary case.

This separation lets the paper answer a reviewer challenge directly. If a local
selector beats a SUDS-composed row, the result is reported as evidence that
local importance can be stronger for that workload and setting. The main
contribution remains the scheduler interface that constrains the allowable
quality budget. The local selector then spends that budget on concrete columns.

\subsection{Pareto Selection Rule}

The selection rule keeps only rows that have measured local accuracy evidence,
are not dominated by measured same-fabric baselines under the EDP and accuracy
tolerances, and either stay inside the selected accuracy budget or expose a full
Pareto tradeoff. The current \texttt{suds\_pareto} row satisfies these
conditions; the older aggressive rows are therefore ablations rather than main
claims.

\section{Architecture Simulator Details}

\subsection{Energy Terms}

The simulator accumulates energy from ADC conversion, DAC/MZM input conversion,
detector/TIA readout, laser power, SRAM movement, optical-link movement,
sideband control, and digital fallback for boundary fabrics that require it.
Fig.~\ref{fig:energy-breakdown} makes this accounting visible rather than
leaving the energy result as a single aggregate number.

\subsection{Latency Terms}

Baseline latency is derived from photonic tile cycles. The simulator then adds
readout-related timing, sideband-group control timing, and boundary multipliers
for alternate fabrics. The latency model is deliberately simple enough to audit;
it is not a replacement for cycle-accurate RTL or a timing-closed hardware
implementation.

\subsection{Area Terms}

The area model combines a photonic tile proxy, converter area scaling,
sideband-control area, global SRAM area, and fabric-specific adjustments for
boundary rows. These values provide architecture context and design-space
filtering signals; they are not layout area.

\subsection{Sensitivity Cases}

The nominal case is the main descriptive surface. The pessimistic case stresses
ADC scaling, memory scaling, optical-link cost, laser cost, control cost, and
digital-fallback cost, and it also reduces pruning compute relief. This stress
test guards against a result that depends on a single optimistic parameter. The
selected Pareto row preserves a positive EDP advantage in that stress case.
The system-sensitivity record extends the check into a parameter sweep over
memory bandwidth, activation reuse, batch size, sequence length, ADC energy,
DAC/MZM energy, laser multiplier, optical-link loss, and sideband-control
overhead. The named nominal, optimistic, and pessimistic regimes preserve the
bounded selected claim, while the extreme DAC/MZM rows are retained as boundary
evidence. The uncertainty record adds bootstrap confidence intervals for the
measured accuracy seeds and Monte Carlo intervals for the target PPA regime; it
also records low-slack, high-memory-pressure,
small-batch, long-sequence, activation-sensitive, and signal-only-dominant
cases where the interface should not be used as a universal policy.

\section{Design-Space Sweep}

\subsection{Sweep Dimensions}

The design-space sweep varies tile dimension, tile count, cores per tile,
sideband group columns, and converter-sharing mode. The selected point is not
chosen because it maximizes a single metric. It is retained because it remains
comparable to the Lightening-style photonic tensor-core surface and uses the
available sideband-control calibration anchor, keeping the comparison narrow
enough for review.

\subsection{Selected Point Rationale}

The \(32 \times 32\) tile preserves the comparison surface used by the
Transformer photonic literature. Four tiles match the LT-B-style reference, two
cores per tile keep the per-tile accumulation point meaningful, and the
32-column sideband group uses the available control calibration. Temporal
accumulation before conversion keeps output-stationary readout in the selected
fabric. Other design points remain in the design-space JSON but are not used as
the main paper setting.

\subsection{Pareto Front Recording}

The design-space files mark Pareto rows over energy, latency, and area within
each workload and condition. This lets a reviewer separate three questions:
whether the selected point is a reasonable local design choice, whether the main
row is Pareto-competitive against measured same-fabric baselines, and whether
\texttt{suds\_pareto} remains nondominated after accounting for the accuracy
budget, EDP, memory pressure, control overhead, and calibration sensitivity. It
satisfies that multi-objective criterion for both active workloads. Raw
energy/latency/area dominators are still recorded, so the paper does not claim
global PPA optimality.

\section{Accuracy Protocol}

\subsection{MPS-Only Execution}

All selected local accuracy runs use the MPS backend; CPU fallback is not used
for selected model evidence. The full MobileViT-S conservative Pareto point
uses eight seeds, and each seed processes the full 50,000-image validation
target. The run JSON files record \texttt{device=mps} and record the MLX
default device as a GPU device. Rows without this metadata are excluded from
the conservative selected surface.

\subsection{BERT GLUE Rows}

The BERT GLUE rows cover SST-2, MRPC, MNLI, QQP, QNLI, and RTE validation
surfaces and are linked to architecture rows through the GLUE linkage CSV. The
selected BERT Pareto point has zero measured primary-metric loss in the
aggregate evidence. The perturbation mechanism used for the selected BERT rows
is binary column zeroing: columns assigned to DEGRADE or PRUNE tiers are zeroed
in the forward pass. Gaussian noise injection is an alternative mechanism that
produces larger, seed-sensitive accuracy drops; it is retained as boundary
evidence, not as the selected perturbation implementation. The schedule link
moves the grounding from analytical slack records to hardware-derived metadata
for the final claim.

\subsection{MobileViT-S Rows}

The MobileViT-S conservative point replaces older aggressive rows whose
accuracy losses were near or above the one-percentage-point target. The
no-prune policy has an aggregate Top-1 delta of \(-0.0145\) percentage points,
a converter-energy ratio of approximately 0.205 relative to dense 8-bit
readout, and a prune ratio of zero. Its role is to define a measured Pareto
point, not the maximum possible energy-reduction point.

\section{Evidence Selection}

\subsection{Same-Fabric Dominance}

The selected row is compared with measured same-fabric baselines using both EDP
and accuracy. For BERT, the selected row has an EDP ratio of about
0.666 and zero accuracy loss. The L1 row has nearly identical accuracy and
slightly lower EDP, but the difference is inside the selection tolerance once
the schedule guard is included. For MobileViT-S, the selected row has an EDP
ratio of about 0.919 and accuracy loss near zero. Several local selectors have
lower EDP, but they lose substantially more accuracy and therefore do not
dominate under the two-dimensional criterion. Alternate-fabric rows with lower
EDP are reported separately as boundary context.

\subsection{Accuracy Budget}

The selected accuracy target is one percentage point. Earlier aggressive
MobileViT-S rows exceeded that limit, so the selected policy changes rather
than treating the old row as selected. The worst selected delta is \(-0.015\)
percentage points, while the older rows remain in the evidence table.

\subsection{Schedule Grounding}

The schedule-grounding evidence links MPS accuracy, MobileViT completion, BERT
schedule metadata, and GLUE schedule linkage. The older analytical slack record
is kept as data-generation history, but the final claim is grounded in a
hardware-derived schedule link in the architecture files.

\section{Limitations}

This paper does not claim a fabricated photonic chip, extracted layout,
foundry-backed circuit signoff, bench-energy measurement, optical-device
signoff, or deployed system. Uniform 4-bit rows remain accuracy boundaries
where the accuracy run is not measured. TeMPO and ASTRA rows are boundary
architecture contexts because they change the conversion or stochastic optical
fabric. Public-reproduction alignment and local gate checks are not external
peer review.

The sensitivity sweep also shows that sufficiently extreme common DAC/MZM cost
can erase the MobileViT-S selected advantage. The claim is therefore bounded to
the named valid memory/conversion/link regimes rather than to universal
photonic conversion dominance. Calibration ranges likewise keep converter and
photonic parameters as calibration and boundary evidence; they do not convert
the simulator into a device solver, extracted layout, silicon measurement, or
bench-energy result. Failure and uncertainty records further bound the
interface: near-deadline low-slack kernels should fall back to keep-all or
disabled control, memory-dominated regimes can reduce the margin, small-batch
and long-sequence expansions require their own measured MPS accuracy before
selection, and signal-only rows remain selector-boundary evidence rather than
proof of universal dominance by the interface.

Additional robustness records make three boundaries explicit. Cross-workload
transfer can degrade MobileViT-S when a BERT-calibrated schedule is reused.
BERT perturbation accuracy under the selected binary-column-zeroing mechanism
is stable, while Gaussian noise injection deltas are sensitive to seed and
scale. DeiT-Tiny measured accuracy loses more than one percentage point under
the tested setting. These records support robustness and boundary labeling, not
universal workload or vision-model applicability.

\section{Conclusion}

SUDS reframes scheduler slack as an explicit budget interface for dynamic
photonic Transformer accelerators. When mapped to a Lightening-style photonic
tensor-core fabric, the interface composes with local selectors, keeps measured
accuracy linkage on BERT-base GLUE and MobileViT-S Transformer blocks, and
maintains a pessimistic system-level EDP advantage over the same-scope
reference. Additional control-plane simulation, GLUE and MobileViT robustness
rows, converter-corner coverage, and transfer/generalization records strengthen
the selected evidence while preserving the same headline claim. The resulting
submission package is intentionally bounded: it presents architecture evidence
clearly, and it is explicit about where that evidence stops.
