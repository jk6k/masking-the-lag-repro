\author{Anonymous Authors}

\begin{abstract}
Dynamic photonic tensor cores can accelerate Transformer attention and
feed-forward kernels, but their system efficiency is constrained by conversion,
memory movement, operand broadcast, and a missing control path between the
software scheduler and accelerator-internal quality knobs. This paper presents
SUDS, a scheduler-derived budget interface for Dynamic Photonic Transformer
accelerators. SUDS exposes timing slack as KEEP, DEGRADE, and PRUNE budgets for
Lightening-style dynamic photonic tensor-core tiles, then composes those
budgets with local L1 or signal-amplitude selectors for ADC precision,
degradation, and pruning decisions. The architecture model maps BERT-base GLUE
encoder kernels and MobileViT-S Transformer blocks to four-tile, two-core
DPTC-style optical arrays and accounts for optical compute, E/O and O/E
conversion, memory movement, optical-link cost, sideband-control overhead, and
sensitivity settings tied to ADC macro, RTL, and PHY boundary artifacts. Across
two Transformer workloads, the selected SUDS operating point keeps governed MPS
accuracy rows linked to architecture-modeled PPA and preserves at least
14.60\% pessimistic EDP improvement versus the same-scope Lightening-style
DPTC reference. The evidence is architecture simulation plus governed MPS
accuracy validation and calibration artifacts. It is not a fabrication, layout,
foundry, circuit-signoff, optical-device-signoff, or bench-energy claim.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Transformer accelerators based on dynamic photonic tensor cores can attack the
matrix multiplications that dominate attention and feed-forward layers. A
Lightening-style DPTC fabric supplies a useful comparison point because it
supports dynamic full-range matrix multiplication rather than only
weight-static optical tensors. HyAtten-style systems sharpen a different
observation: conversion is often the limiting term, so low-resolution
converters and local signal selectors matter. These mechanisms still leave a
control-interface gap. The scheduler already knows when a kernel has timing
slack, but converter precision, degradation, and pruning choices are usually
selected by local model or signal proxies alone.

SUDS turns that timing information into a resource-control budget. The
scheduler exports per-layer and per-column slack to the accelerator control
plane. The photonic tile controller maps slack to KEEP, DEGRADE, and PRUNE
budgets. Local selectors then choose the exact columns inside those budgets.
This framing is important: SUDS is not a claim that slack alone is a semantic
importance oracle. It is a budget interface that composes scheduler pressure
with local L1, signal-amplitude, or overflow-style selectors.

This manuscript is the active architecture-first route for IEEE TETC. The
contribution stack is restricted to architecture modeling, measured MPS
accuracy linkage, and calibration evidence.

\section{Architecture Model}

\subsection{Transformer Kernel Mapping}

The simulator represents each Transformer operation as a tiled matrix
multiplication on a DPTC array. BERT-base uses the 12-layer encoder at sequence
length 128 and includes QKV projection, QK score, attention-value, output
projection, and two FFN kernels per layer. MobileViT-S uses the repository's
Transformer-block operation manifest and promotes the linear attention and FFN
kernels under \texttt{global\_rep}. Each GEMM is lowered into DPTC tile work,
with latency and energy accumulated over optical compute, DAC/MZM,
detector/TIA, ADC, laser, SRAM movement, optical-link movement, and
sideband-control groups.

\subsection{Selected Operating Point}

The selected operating point intentionally stays close to the Lightening and
HyAtten comparison surface. It uses 32 by 32 DPTC tiles, four tiles, two cores
per tile, 32-column sideband groups, and temporal accumulation before ADC. The
design-space artifact sweeps 4536 configurations and identifies 594 Pareto
rows over raw energy, latency, and area. The R5 multi-objective audit then
joins this sweep to the measured accuracy and same-simulator baseline gates.
It records that the selected point is not an unconstrained raw-PPA optimum;
instead, it is the governed Pareto point used after applying the 1 pp
accuracy budget, R3/R4 evidence linkage, memory-pressure, control-overhead,
and calibration-sensitivity objectives. Table~\ref{tab:operating-point}
records the promoted setting.
The reinforced R5 selection-rationale artifact adds a selected-neighborhood
audit and records raw-PPA dominators as explicit pressure against any global
PPA-optimality wording. Those variants improve one or more raw PPA axes, but
they do not replace the promoted point because the promoted claim is tied to
the governed accuracy, fairness, memory-pressure, control, and calibration
surface.

\subsection{SUDS Control Layer}

For each scheduled kernel, the simulator records issue time, tile latency,
downstream deadline, and normalized slack. SUDS maps slack to a tier budget:
low slack keeps high-resolution conversion, medium slack degrades to
low-resolution conversion, and high slack permits pruning or stronger
degradation. SUDS+L1 and SUDS+signal keep the scheduler-derived tier counts but
use local selectors to choose exact columns. SUDS-only is retained as an
ablation that isolates the budget interface from the local selector. The
science gate promotes only the schedule-guarded \texttt{suds\_pareto} row as
the main SUDS claim; the more aggressive SUDS+L1 and SUDS+signal rows remain
visible ablations and boundary context.

\subsection{Pessimistic Gate}

The fixed architecture gate stresses ADC scaling, memory movement,
optical-link penalties, control overhead, and reduced compute relief from
pruning. Under that setting, the selected SUDS point preserves 22.36\%
energy and 25.81\% EDP improvement for BERT-base GLUE, and 14.75\% energy
and 14.60\% EDP improvement for MobileViT-S Transformer blocks, versus the
same-scope Lightening-style reference. The R6 system-sensitivity audit then

ADC/DAC cost, laser multiplier, optical-link loss, and sideband overhead. Its
named pessimistic regime preserves at least 12.10\% EDP improvement, while
extreme one-at-a-time DAC/MZM scaling exposes the MobileViT-S boundary where
the promoted point is no longer beneficial.
The reinforced R6 artifact also adds combined stress grids over conversion,
link, memory, sequence length, ADC energy, and sideband overhead, plus stacked

boundaries; they are not promoted as realistic operating regimes.

\section{Problem Statement and Claim Contract}

\subsection{Core Research Question}

The core research question is: given scheduler slack, a Transformer kernel
DAG, and the current state of a DPTC-style photonic tile array, can the control
plane choose KEEP, DEGRADE, and PRUNE budgets that minimize modeled system
energy while satisfying kernel deadlines, resource constraints, calibration
boundaries, and measured accuracy guards?

This question intentionally defines SUDS as an architecture interface rather
than as an empirical selector rule. The scheduler supplies deadline pressure.
SUDS emits a budget. A local selector then spends that budget on exact columns.
The output of SUDS is therefore a budget vector, not a final mask.

\subsection{Deadline-Constrained Energy Minimization}

Let \(G=(V,E)\) be the scheduled Transformer kernel DAG. Each node
\(i \in V\) is a GEMM-like kernel with dimensions \(m_i,d_i,n_i\), release
time \(r_i\), deadline \(D_i\), normalized scheduler slack \(\sigma_i\), and
a controllable set of \(C_i\) output columns or column groups. Let \(X_t\)
denote the state of tile resource \(t\): tile dimension, tile availability,
core count, sideband grouping, ADC-sharing mode, memory/link state, and
calibrated cost parameters. SUDS chooses
\[
B_i = (N_i^{\mathrm{K}},N_i^{\mathrm{D}},N_i^{\mathrm{P}}),
\]
the KEEP, DEGRADE, and PRUNE budget for kernel \(i\). The local selector
\(\psi\) chooses the exact columns inside that budget.

The architecture problem is:
\begin{equation}
\min_{\{B_i,s_i,Y_i\}}
\sum_{i \in V} E_i(B_i,X_{Y_i},\mathcal{P})
\label{eq:suds-energy-objective}
\end{equation}
subject to the schedule, budget, and evidence constraints below. Here
\(s_i\) is the start time, \(Y_i\) is the tile-resource assignment, and
\(\mathcal{P}\) is the calibrated parameter set. The energy term includes ADC,
DAC/MZM, detector/TIA, laser, SRAM movement, optical-link movement,
sideband-control, and digital-fallback terms. The paper reports EDP as an
audit metric, but the problem being solved is deadline-constrained energy
minimization.

\begin{align}
s_j &\ge s_i + L_i(B_i,X_{Y_i}), && \forall (i,j)\in E \label{eq:precedence}\\
s_i + L_i(B_i,X_{Y_i}) &\le D_i, && \forall i \in V \label{eq:deadline}\\
N_i^{\mathrm{K}}+N_i^{\mathrm{D}}+N_i^{\mathrm{P}} &= C_i, && \forall i \in V \label{eq:budget}\\
B_i &\in \mathcal{B}(\sigma_i,X_{Y_i},\mathcal{P}), && \forall i \in V \label{eq:slack-budget}\\
R_w(\{B_i\},\psi) &\le \epsilon_w, && \forall w \in \mathcal{W}_{\mathrm{prom}} .
\label{eq:accuracy-risk}
\end{align}

Equation~\ref{eq:precedence} preserves DAG order.
Equation~\ref{eq:deadline} is the deadline constraint.
Equation~\ref{eq:budget} makes the SUDS output a conserved budget over
columns or column groups.
Equation~\ref{eq:slack-budget} encodes the slack-to-budget policy: low slack
raises the required KEEP budget, while higher slack permits more DEGRADE or
PRUNE budget. If a workload evidence surface does not permit pruning, the
feasible set forces \(N_i^{\mathrm{P}}=0\). The promoted MobileViT-S Pareto
point uses that zero-prune guard.
Equation~\ref{eq:accuracy-risk} blocks headline promotion when the measured
accuracy risk exceeds the workload guard.

\subsection{Formal Input and Output}

The formal inputs are scheduler slack, kernel DAG, and tile state.
Scheduler slack carries deadline pressure after DPTC mapping.
The kernel DAG carries operation order, dimensions, release times, and
deadlines.
Tile state carries available photonic resources, conversion sharing, sideband
grouping, memory/link state, and calibration parameters.

The formal output is the budget \(B_i\).
KEEP means high-resolution conversion is preserved.
DEGRADE means lower-resolution or degraded readout is allowed.
PRUNE means removal or condensation is allowed only when the evidence surface
permits it.
The exact column mask is deliberately outside the SUDS output contract.
It is produced by the local selector inside the budget.

\subsection{Why a Scheduler Interface is Needed}

The motivating problem is not only that optical multiplication can be fast.
The motivating problem is that a Transformer accelerator must move between
digital scheduling decisions and photonic conversion resources.
In a DPTC-style fabric, the optical datapath can expose high arithmetic
parallelism, yet conversion and memory terms can dominate the system-level
benefit.
The scheduler sees queueing pressure, layer order, tile occupancy, and
deadline slack.
The converter-control plane sees local column magnitudes and readout limits.
Without an interface, these two views remain separated.
SUDS is the interface layer between those views.
It does not replace local importance metrics.
It restricts the budget that local metrics are allowed to spend.

This distinction matters for the TETC pivot.
If the method were presented as a pure slack selector, then a reviewer could
rightly ask why L1 or signal-only rows are not the main method.
If the method were presented as a pure signal selector, then the scheduler
would disappear and the architecture contribution would collapse into an
existing local heuristic.
The evidence now promotes a third position.
The scheduler supplies a hardware-derived budget.
The local selector chooses columns inside that budget.
The paper claims the interface and the measured operating point, not
universal dominance over every local selector.

\subsection{Claim Units}

The claim unit in this paper is an architecture row.
An architecture row combines a workload, a DPTC operating point, a control
policy, a measured or bounded accuracy source, and a modeled PPA accounting
surface.
Rows are not interchangeable across evidence labels.
A measured MPS accuracy row can support accuracy retention.
An architecture-modeled PPA row can support energy, latency, area, and EDP
reasoning.
An ADC macro row can support a parameter value.
An RTL synthesis proxy can support a sideband-control parameter.
A PHY sweep can support an optical-link boundary setting.
None of these rows alone supports a fabricated-chip conclusion.

The main promoted condition is \texttt{suds\_pareto}.
For BERT-base GLUE, \texttt{suds\_pareto} uses the measured zero-loss L1
condition under the DPTC schedule guard.
For MobileViT-S, \texttt{suds\_pareto} uses the measured no-prune
signal-overflow condition with \(\tau_{\mathrm{low}}=0.30\) and
\(\tau_{\mathrm{high}}=0.95\).
The older \texttt{suds\_l1} and \texttt{suds\_signal} rows are retained as
ablations because they show what happens when the budget is more aggressive.
They are not hidden.
They are not relabeled.
They are not used as the main submission claim.

\subsection{Evidence Labels}

The manuscript uses explicit evidence labels.
\texttt{measured\_mps\_glue} means that GLUE validation was evaluated through
the governed local MPS backend.
\texttt{measured\_mps\_imagenet} means that MobileViT-S ImageNet validation
was evaluated through the governed local MPS backend.
\texttt{modeled\_system\_ppa} means that the PPA numbers come from the
architecture simulator.
\texttt{ngspice\_or\_fallback\_macro\_calibration} means that the ADC tier
values are calibration values for the simulator.
\texttt{rtl\_synthesis\_proxy} means that sideband-control energy is tied to

\texttt{parametric\_boundary} means that a parameter stress case is bounded by
a sweep rather than by a physical implementation.

These labels are intentionally repeated in the artifacts.
They make the paper less rhetorically smooth, but they make it harder to
overclaim.
For a journal submission, that is the right tradeoff.

It does not report a circuit-signoff result.

\section{Scheduler-Derived Metadata}

\subsection{Kernel Schedule Construction}

The BERT-base schedule is generated from the canonical encoder structure.
Each of the 12 layers contributes QKV projection, QK score, attention-value
context, attention output projection, FFN expansion, and FFN projection
kernels.
At sequence length 128 this creates a deterministic GEMM schedule.
The MobileViT-S schedule is extracted from the local operation manifest.
Only Transformer-block linear operations under the \texttt{global\_rep}
surface are promoted into the Transformer workload.
Convolutional support layers remain outside the promoted DPTC Transformer
kernel accounting.

For each GEMM, the simulator records matrix dimensions \(m\), \(d\), and
\(n\).
The mapping computes tile counts along the three dimensions.
It derives the number of DPTC tiles, output groups, output values, operand
values, and base latency.
It also assigns a schedule start time and a downstream deadline.
The normalized slack value is then derived from the schedule window rather
than from an analytical layer index alone.
The architecture JSON records the field
\texttt{slack\_source=dptc\_photonic\_tile\_schedule}.

\subsection{From Analytical Provenance to Schedule Metadata}

The original GLUE measured artifact retains analytical slack provenance.
That provenance is useful because it records how the earlier GLUE rows were
generated.
It is not sufficient for the final TETC claim.
The science gate therefore requires an additional hardware-derived schedule
link.
The current architecture artifact supplies 1008 BERT schedule-kernel rows.
It also supplies 126 GLUE linkage rows.
The linkage rows connect measured GLUE rows to architecture conditions,
including \texttt{suds\_pareto}.
This is why the S3 gate passes while still reporting that the original GLUE
slack field was analytical.

The R2 scheduler-trace artifact strengthens that link by replaying the
architecture rows through five scheduler variants: FIFO, ASAP,
EDF/deadline-aware, utilization-aware, and SUDS-aware. The promoted
\texttt{suds\_pareto} rows use SUDS-aware hardware-derived schedule trace
IDs \texttt{r2\_7b7427345a30be24} for BERT-base GLUE and
\texttt{r2\_e1d13a0a1855de8b} for MobileViT-S Transformer blocks. The same
artifact records that scheduler distributions are distinguishable, with a
maximum signature distance of 0.566732 against a 0.010000 stop threshold.
This makes the promoted slack source an emitted scheduler trace rather than
only a column in the architecture summary table.

The R3 end-to-end perturbation artifact then checks the accuracy side of that
same contract. It links the promoted BERT-base GLUE and MobileViT-S
\texttt{suds\_pareto} rows to measured MPS forward passes, verifies that their
accuracy-source tier ratios match the architecture PPA tier ratios with maximum
policy-ratio delta 0.000000, and records the promoted trace IDs
\texttt{r2\_7b7427345a30be24} and \texttt{r2\_e1d13a0a1855de8b}. The worst
promoted accuracy delta is -0.014500 percentage points, inside the one
percentage-point target. This artifact is still deliberately bounded: it is a
model-forward tiered perturbation audit, not a bit-exact ADC or optical-device
simulation.

\subsection{Schedule Metadata Fields}

Each schedule row includes the workload name.
Each row includes the kernel identifier.
Each row includes the kernel class.
Each row includes the layer index.
Each row includes matrix dimensions.
Each row includes DPTC tile counts.
Each row includes output groups.
Each row includes output values.

The R2 scheduler trace additionally records scheduler variant, schedule trace

\subsection{Why the Schedule is Hardware-Derived}

The schedule is hardware-derived because the slack is computed after mapping
the kernel to the selected tile shape and parallel-core count.
Changing tile dimension changes tile counts.
Changing tile count changes parallelism.
Changing cores per tile changes cycles.
Changing sideband grouping changes control accounting.
Changing ADC sharing changes conversion timing and energy.
The schedule therefore depends on the modeled architecture point.
It is not only a static layer-order heuristic.
R2 also shows that scheduler choice changes the slack distribution. SUDS-aware
improves low-tail slack over FIFO for the promoted rows, while a
utilization-aware scheduler has the highest p10 slack in the scheduler-only
ablation. The paper therefore claims the scheduler-budget interface and the
measured promoted operating point, not universal dominance by the SUDS-aware
scheduler over every possible scheduling objective.

\section{SUDS Policy Definition}

\subsection{Budget Semantics}

SUDS maps normalized slack into three budget classes.
KEEP preserves high-resolution conversion.
DEGRADE assigns lower-resolution conversion.
PRUNE permits removal or condensation when the evidence surface allows it.
The promoted MobileViT-S policy uses zero pruning.
That choice is deliberate.
The earlier aggressive policy reduced ADC energy more strongly, but it lost
too much accuracy and was dominated by stronger local baselines.
The conservative policy sacrifices some EDP improvement in exchange for a
measured accuracy delta near zero.

\subsection{Local Selector Composition}

The budget interface determines how many columns may be assigned to each tier.
The local selector determines which columns are assigned.
For L1 composition, the selector prioritizes columns by weight magnitude.
For signal composition, the selector uses signal-amplitude or overflow-style
evidence.
For slack-only composition, the selector is essentially the schedule budget
itself.
For random composition, the selector is a same-sparsity boundary check.

This separation lets the paper answer a reviewer challenge directly.
If a local selector beats the SUDS composition, the result is not hidden.
The result shows that local importance can be stronger at that point.
The main SUDS contribution remains the interface that lets the scheduler
shape the allowable quality budget.

\subsection{Pareto Promotion Rule}

The science gate promotes only rows that satisfy three properties.
First, the row must have measured MPS accuracy evidence.
Second, the row must not be dominated by measured same-fabric baselines under
the gate's EDP and accuracy tolerances.
Third, the row must stay inside the promoted accuracy budget or expose a full
Pareto tradeoff instead of a single headline.
The current \texttt{suds\_pareto} row satisfies these conditions.
The older aggressive rows do not satisfy them as main claims.

\section{Expanded Related Work}

\subsection{Dynamic Photonic Tensor Cores}

Lightening-Transformer is the closest same-fabric reference point.
It is important because it targets dynamic full-range Transformer matrix
multiplication rather than only static-weight CNN-style optical tensors.
The selected SUDS DPTC fabric uses this row as the same-scope reference.
The paper does not claim to reproduce Lightening's physical implementation.
It uses a Lightening-style architecture parameterization and boundary.

\subsection{Conversion-Aware Attention Acceleration}

HyAtten motivates conversion-aware photonic attention acceleration.
Its central lesson for this paper is that signal conversion can dominate the
benefit of photonic compute.
SUDS borrows that bottleneck framing but not the implementation.
The HyAtten-style row is therefore a boundary row.
It is a reviewer-facing context row, not a hidden baseline.

\subsection{Time-Multiplexed Photonic Cores}

TeMPO studies time-multiplexed dynamic photonic tensor acceleration with
device, circuit, and architecture customization.
The selected SUDS row does not claim TeMPO's device path.
The TeMPO-style row in the simulator is a boundary fabric.
It helps a reviewer see whether the SUDS DPTC point remains sensible when
alternate readout assumptions are present.

\subsection{Stochastic Photonic Transformers}

ASTRA studies stochastic optical Transformer acceleration.
Its stochastic readout and multiplier assumptions differ from the selected
DPTC fabric.
The ASTRA-style row is therefore not a same-scope baseline for SUDS.
It is an alternate-fabric boundary that pressures the claim.

\subsection{Photonic-Aware Compression}

ENLighten studies sparse and low-rank Transformer compression for photonic
tensor cores.
That work is adjacent because it changes model structure to fit photonic
resources.
SUDS instead changes the runtime budget interface.
The two ideas are compatible in principle.
This manuscript does not claim their combined result.

\subsection{Optical Transformer Scaling}

Optical Transformer studies motivate why Transformer linear operations are
interesting targets for optical acceleration.
They also show that analog optical advantages depend heavily on conversion,
noise, and scaling assumptions.
This paper follows that caution by separating measured accuracy from modeled
PPA.

\subsection{Non-Coherent Transformer Photonics}

TRON represents an earlier non-coherent Transformer photonic accelerator
direction.
It motivates the broader photonic Transformer space but does not define the
same DPTC fabric used here.
The manuscript cites it as background rather than as a matched baseline.

\subsection{Sparse Photonic Neural Acceleration}

SONIC and CrossLight show that sparsity, cross-layer co-design, and photonic
mapping have a long history in neural accelerators.
SUDS differs by tying the resource-control budget to a Transformer schedule.
The local selector can still be magnitude-aware or signal-aware.
The scheduler interface is the new part.

\subsection{Digital Transformer and Mobile Baselines}

BERT and GLUE define the NLP validation surface.
MobileViT defines the compact vision-Transformer-family workload.
The paper uses these workloads to avoid a single-model claim.
The results are still not universal.
They cover one NLP encoder surface and one compact vision Transformer surface.

\section{Limitations}

This paper does not claim a fabricated photonic chip, extracted layout,
foundry closure, bench-energy measurement, optical-device signoff, or deployed
system. Uniform 4-bit rows remain accuracy boundaries where the accuracy run is
not measured. TeMPO and ASTRA rows are boundary architecture contexts because
they change the conversion or stochastic optical fabric. Full external red-team
review remains preferred before submission, even though the local G1 gate now
has an internal substitute and a public-repro alignment check.
The R6 sensitivity sweep also shows that sufficiently extreme common DAC/MZM
cost can erase the MobileViT-S promoted advantage, so the claim is bounded to
the named valid memory/conversion/link regimes rather than universal photonic
conversion dominance.
The R8 calibration-range artifact similarly keeps ADC and photonic parameter
ranges at calibration and boundary level; it does not convert the simulator into
device-solver, foundry, extracted-layout, silicon, or bench-energy evidence.
The R10 failure suite further bounds use of the interface: near-deadline
low-slack kernels should fall back to keep-all or disabled control,
memory-dominated regimes can reduce the margin, small-batch and long-sequence
expansions require their own measured MPS accuracy before promotion, and
signal-only rows remain selector-boundary evidence rather than proof of
universal SUDS dominance.

\section{Conclusion}

SUDS reframes scheduler slack as an explicit budget interface for dynamic
photonic Transformer accelerators. When mapped to a Lightening-style DPTC
fabric, the interface composes with L1 and signal selectors, preserves
governed MPS accuracy linkage on BERT-base GLUE and MobileViT-S Transformer
blocks, and maintains a pessimistic system-level EDP advantage versus the
same-scope DPTC reference. The result is a bounded TETC architecture package:
strong enough to promote as architecture evidence, and explicit about where
the evidence stops.
